Definition

There are slightly different variants of the definitions. We follow essentially Strichartz's definitions as defined in his excellent book (p. 147) [1].

Definition: Big-oh for finite quantities

Definition (Big oh for finite quantities)

Let x0∈Rx_0\in\mathbb{R}x0​∈R and fff and ggg are real valued functions defined near x0x_0x0​. If there exist (fixed) constants C>0C>0C>0 and δ>0\delta>0δ>0 such that
∣f(x)∣≤C∣g(x)∣for all x∈(x0−δ,x0+δ),|f(x)|\le C |g(x)| \quad \text{for all } x \in (x_0 - \delta, x_0 + \delta),∣f(x)∣≤C∣g(x)∣for all x∈(x0​−δ,x0​+δ),
or,
∣f(x)∣∣g(x)∣≤Cfor all x∈(x0−δ,x0+δ)\{x0},\frac{|f(x)|}{|g(x)|}\le C \quad \text{for all } x\in (x_0 - \delta, x_0 + \delta)\backslash\{x_0\},∣g(x)∣∣f(x)∣​≤Cfor all x∈(x0​−δ,x0​+δ)\{x0​},
then we write
f(x)=O ⁣(g(x))asx→x0,f(x)={\mathcal{O}\!\left( g(x) \right)} \quad\text{as}\quad x\to x_0,f(x)=O(g(x))asx→x0​,

and say f(x) is a big oh of g(x)g(x)g(x) near x0x_0x0​ or f(x) is of order of g(x)g(x)g(x) near x0x_0x0​.

Definition: Big-oh for growing quantities

Definition (Big oh for growing quantities)

Let fff and ggg are real valued functions defined on near ∞\infty∞ (i.e., [N,∞)[N,\infty)[N,∞) for some N∈R∪{−∞}N\in\R \cup \{-\infty\}N∈R∪{−∞}). If there exist (fixed) constants C>0C>0C>0 and M>0M>0M>0 such that

Then, the big-oh condition says that the magnitude of fff can be controlled or bounded by ggg.

Interpretation (error form of big-oh)

The error form is the most common form of big-oh (e.g., f(x)=g(x)+O ⁣(h(x))f(x) = g(x) + {\mathcal{O}\!\left( h(x) \right)}f(x)=g(x)+O(h(x)) as x→x0x\to x_0x→x0​). Intuitively, we can interpret it as follows:

  • the error caused by g(x)g(x)g(x) in place of f(x)f(x)f(x) is no worse than h(x)h(x)h(x) (up to constant multiple), or
  • f(x) is made up of g(x)g(x)g(x) and something that behaves like h(x)h(x)h(x) (up to constant multiple), which is assumed to be small.

Remarks

Remark: Advantage of big-oh

Remark (Advantage of big-oh)

Big-oh notation allows us to forget about unimportant details (i.e., exact behaviors of a function) and to focus on what really matters (i.e., what is the magnitude of the function like) when it is helpful.

Remark: Big-oh term may or may not matter

Remark (Big-oh term may or may not matter)

Sometimes big-oh term is the one that matters. This is typically true for growing quanitities. Sometimes, big-oh term is the one that is negligible. This is usually the case for finite quantities.

Remark: Big-oh is about inequality, not equality

Warning (Big-oh is about inequality, not equality)

Consider f(x)=x3f(x)=x^3f(x)=x3 and g(x)=x2g(x)=x^2g(x)=x2 around x0=0x_0=0x0​=0. If xxx is close enough to x0=0x_0=0x0​=0, say δ=0.1\delta = 0.1δ=0.1 (hence x∈(−0.1,0.1)x\in(-0.1, 0.1)x∈(−0.1,0.1)), we have
∣f(x)∣=∣x∣3≤0.1∣x∣2.|f(x)|=|x|^3 \le 0.1|x|^2.∣f(x)∣=∣x∣3≤0.1∣x∣2.

Using big-oh notation, it follows

f(x)=O ⁣(g(x)),f(x) = {\mathcal{O}\!\left( g(x) \right)},f(x)=O(g(x)),

with the constant C=0.1>0C=0.1>0C=0.1>0 appearing in the definition.

However, we do not have "g(x)=O ⁣(f(x))g(x) = {\mathcal{O}\!\left( f(x) \right)}g(x)=O(f(x)) as x→0x\to 0x→0."

To see this, observe that it is impossible to choose a fixed constant C>0C>0C>0 that satisfies "for all x∈(−0.1,0.1)x\in(-0.1, 0.1)x∈(−0.1,0.1), ∣x∣2≤C∣x∣3|x|^2 \le C|x|^3∣x∣2≤C∣x∣3": taking smaller and smaller xxx's, C∣x∣3C|x|^3C∣x∣3 eventually becomes smaller than ∣x∣2|x|^2∣x∣2 no matter how big CCC you choose.

Even if we try different δ≠0.1\delta\neq 0.1δ=0.1, the situation is not changed. This is because the violation of big-oh condition (i.e., inequality flip) happens due to xxx's that are close to 0. Thus we conclude

g(x)≠O ⁣(f(x))asx→0.g(x) \neq {\mathcal{O}\!\left( f(x) \right)} \quad\text{as}\quad x\to 0.g(x)=O(f(x))asx→0.

Observe, however, as you can see from the above reasoning, big-oh encapsulates inequalities.

Summary

  • Big 'oh' notation allows us to focus only on the most important term out of (possibly) infinitely many terms. (See Advantage of big-oh)

    • In some cases, all terms except the big-oh term matter. (See Example: Big-oh notation with exponential)
    • In other cases, only the big-oh term matters. (See Example: Big-oh notation for complexity of matrix multiplication)

  • Big 'oh' is essentially inequality. It is not an equality, though its notation uses '===' sign. (See Remark: Big-oh is about inequality, not equality)

  • Eventually, people use big-oh notation at an intuitive level. But it is good to see it works rigorously as well to convince yourself. (See Appendix for some rigorous approaches)
